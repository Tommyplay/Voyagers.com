Scientists from NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) in Pasadena, California, announced today the discovery of a new moon orbiting Neptune.
The moon was discovered on Wednesday, July 5, when photos returned from the Voyager 2 probe en route to Neptune showed it. The new moon,
dubbed 1989 N1, was first detected in Voyager 2 photographs beamed to Earth in mid-June. Images of the small body in its expected orbit later proved its existence.
Dr. Stephen P. Synnott of JPL's Voyager imaging team discovered the faint, bright smudge in Voyager images that led to the moon's discovery.
The new Neptunian satellite, according to Synnott, could have a diameter of 200 to 600 kilometers (approximately 125 to 400 miles) and circles around Neptune.
